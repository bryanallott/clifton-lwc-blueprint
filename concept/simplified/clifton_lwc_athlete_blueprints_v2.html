Sprint athletes need to be ready to race multiple times per day across both individual and relay events. Beach Flags is the outlier - a flags winner at LWC has run somewhere between 12 and 20 rounds by the time they're done, which is a very different fatigue profile than the sprint. The relay adds a team component and another race to the day. Coaches should expect 4 efforts per session minimum for athletes in both sprint and flags, and flag athletes progressing deep into the draw need active recovery and mental composure between rounds, not just physical freshness.

The journey through the phases
Phase 1 is honest rest dressed up as cross-training - the body needs a break after the season. Phase 2 builds the aerobic platform that most sprint athletes skip, which is the mistake. Soft sand running and posterior chain work here mean Phase 3 threshold work actually sticks. By Phase 4 you're running 90m repeats at race pace with full recovery, building toward 12 reps, sharpening starts and turning mechanics. Phase 5 cuts the volume in half, keeps the quality, and trusts the work that's already been done.

Ocean Swimmer
The ocean swim is often where races are decided - or given away. This program builds from easy technique work and cross-training into progressively harder open-water volume, peaking at 8 × 400m before the taper brings it back sharp.

On paper this looks like the lightest competition load of any discipline - 3 swims for individuals, a relay on top. But ocean swims at LWC are not pool swims. Conditions in PE in November can be serious - surf, current, navigation - and a hard 3-race campaign in real ocean leaves athletes far more depleted than the numbers suggest. Female athletes nominated for the mixed ocean relay face a third event commitment on top of individual and team swims. Coaches should plan for genuine recovery between ocean days, not just between sessions. Athletes who underestimate ocean fatigue get found out in finals.

The journey through the phases
Phase 1 is about feel - drills, easy ocean swims, shoulder stability work. Nothing is timed yet. Phase 2 introduces real volume, long open-water sessions of 1500–3000m and aerobic pool pyramid sets. Sighting and stroke tempo become habits. Phase 3 brings CSS threshold sets and race-pace repeats - this is where the engine gets built properly. Phase 4 is the business: 8 × 400m open water at goal pace, once a week. Phase 5 halves the volume, keeps the quality, and adds a short open-water session to practice sighting the comp course if possible.

Ski Paddler
Ocean ski racing rewards aerobic engine size, paddle efficiency, and the ability to hold pace through whatever the ocean throws at you. The program builds a long aerobic base, moves into threshold intervals, then peaks at 8 × 1000m before tapering sharp.
Expected Competition Load at LWC
Male
Ocean Ski

Female
Ocean Ski
3 races
Ski races at LWC are long and technically demanding - ocean conditions, surf launches, and buoy rounding all factor in. Junior males face 4 races to get through the draw; junior females, 3. That's a meaningful difference in total paddling volume across the championship. The individual nature of ski means there's no relay buffer - every race counts and there's no teammate to compensate. Athletes need to be able to produce race-quality effort on consecutive days, which is why Phase 4 builds toward the 8 × 1000m set: it simulates the cumulative load, not just a single race peak.

Phase 2 introduces long aerobic paddles (60–90 min zone 2) and interval work, always with a stroke-efficiency focus. Phase 3 moves into 1000m threshold repeats, building toward the target set. Board Paddler
Prone board paddling rewards pulling strength, wave intelligence, and the ability to hold race pace over 600m efforts. This program builds from off-board conditioning into long aerobic paddles, through threshold rep work, up to 8 × 600m race simulations.

Board paddlers carry a double event commitment - individual board race and board rescue - and those two events have different demands. The board race is a solo speed effort; rescue adds a partner component and a different pacing strategy. Combined, athletes in both events face 7 starts across the campaign, many of them in open surf conditions. The A & B final structure in board rescue means even teams that don't make the A final race again - there's no sitting out. Coaches should flag that the cumulative paddling volume at LWC is higher than any single training week, and plan recovery accordingly between event days.

Phase 1 keeps the board mostly in the shed - shoulder stability, easy flat-water technique paddles, and surfing as honest cross-training for wave reading and balance. Pool Rescue
Pool rescue athletes live in a world of short, explosive efforts with very little margin for error. Tube rescue, manikin events, and the rescue medley all demand technical precision at race speed - and the competition draw means they do it repeatedly across the week.

Pool rescue at LWC is deceptively demanding. Three races sounds manageable - but these aren't just swim races. Tube rescue is a paired event requiring synchronised execution under pressure, and mistakes in the sequence that go unnoticed in training get penalised or disqualified on the world stage. The A & B final structure means no team sits out regardless of heat result - every pair races again. The training program deliberately builds to 12 × 50m race-pace reps in Phase 4 because the total accumulated pool rescue effort across the draw is closer to that number than athletes expect. Technical perfection under fatigue is the whole game here, and it has to be earned in Phase 3 - errors at race pace need to be ironed out well before comp week.

The journey through the phases
Phase 1 is slow and deliberate - drill sets, event familiarisation at easy pace, shoulder health work. The rescue sequences should be known perfectly before speed is added. Phase 2 builds aerobic volume through main sets and event simulation, with fin speed introduced. The manikin carry and rescue medley get dedicated repetition here. Phase 3 is where race pace enters - short fast reps, full event sequences at speed, and fin work across multiple distances. Phase 4 is the 12 × 50m target set, full event heat simulations from the start block, and race-pace fin sprints. Phase 5 cuts volume sharply, runs a single full dry-run of each event sequence, works starts and turns for the hundredths that matter, and takes a full rest day before comp.

Iron / All-round
Three events, three engines, one body. The Iron athlete has to swim, paddle, and sprint on the beach - often in the same heat. The taper has to be genuinely smart about which event you prioritise.

This is where the competition load picture gets real. An all-round athlete nominated across board, swim, sprint, and relay events is looking at up to 17–19 individual starts across the championship for males, and females in the mixed ocean relay face an additional commitment on top of that. That is not a small number. No specialist at LWC carries this kind of volume. The training program's mandatory recovery weeks in Phase 4 exist precisely because of this - athletes who arrive at Worlds having never experienced multi-event fatigue will be in trouble by day three. Coaches need to be deliberate about which events each athlete is actually nominated for, and the Phase 5 taper instruction to sharpen your strongest scoring event first is the right call. You cannot peak everything. Transitions are where iron races are won - but finishing the last sprint leg is where they're lost if fatigue management has been poor across the week.

Final
The journey through the phases
Phase 1 is a rotation - one session each of ocean swim, beach run, and gym. No event specificity yet, because you need rest more than volume at this point. Phase 2 builds all three engines in parallel: long ocean swims, long paddle sessions alternating ski and board, and easy soft-sand aerobic running. Phase 3 introduces event-specific intensity - CSS swim sets, paddle reps, sprint work, and one swim-to-paddle transition session per week because that's where races are actually won. Phase 4 is race simulation: iron sequences, peak sets rotated by event, beach sprint repeats toward 8–10 × 90m, and a mandatory recovery week every third week. Phase 5 is about knowing your team's points - sharpen the event that matters most, touch the others, run the race in your head, and take two full days off before you compete.
